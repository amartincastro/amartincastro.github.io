00 Overview

Army aviation operations run out of dozens of facilities — battalions, schoolhouses, AASF sites — all doing the same daily work: scheduling flights, tracking readiness, managing flight hours, reporting up the chain. For decades most of it happened on paper and whiteboards.

Aviation Status Dashboard (ASD) replaced those processes — one tool for flight scheduling, maintenance, aircrew training, flying hours, AFTP, and fiscal tracking. Aviation Max sits above it, aggregating ASD data across facilities for enterprise-wide readiness.

02 Boots on the ground

The work didn't start in a design tool. It started in hangars. The way you find out how an Army aviation unit actually runs is to show up — watch what gets written on a whiteboard, handed over on a notecard at shift change, photocopied fourteen times because finance needs a paper trail.

Requirements gathering at the aircraft — Chinook hangar (left) and a UH-60 medevac unit (right), whose motto reads "When I have your wounded." Mockups don't survive a conversation with the people who actually run the flight schedule.

Dozens of site visits over eight years — hangars, ops desks, maintenance bays, training units. Each one fed the product: workflows you can't invent from a brief, edge cases only visible in the field, and quiet requirements ("we already do it this way and we're not going to change") that shaped how the UI had to behave to be adopted.

03 From paper to pixels

The flagship win was flight scheduling. We replaced notecards and whiteboards with software that handled the whole operation: aircraft assigned to missions, aircrew assigned to aircraft, flight hours accruing against training plans and budget, status visible to leadership in real time instead of "let me walk over to ops and ask."

The design problem wasn't the data model. It was replacing a physical artifact the unit was already good at — a whiteboard everyone could see, write on, erase — with a screen as fast for schedulers and as glanceable for passersby. Slow software gets ignored. Ignored software gets replaced with another whiteboard.

The rest of the platform extended outward: AFTP processing, equipment readiness, fiscal administration, status reporting — each one its own paper trail, each one reshaped into an ASD module by the people who'd been doing it by hand.

04 From local automation to enterprise readiness

ASD won unit by unit. Once dozens of facilities were running it, the question changed: commands overseeing aviation readiness across many units didn't need a single facility's schedule — they needed to look across all of them at once.

Aviation Max is the answer. Aggregates readiness data across organizations, preserves local ASD effectiveness, makes the broader picture visible at enterprise scale. Same data spine, different audience, different decisions.

05 Outcome
  • Dozens of U.S. Army flight facilities running ASD as their primary operations platform.
  • Thousands of soldiers using it daily — schedulers, maintainers, aircrew, command staff.
  • Tens of thousands of missions and flights scheduled, tracked, and reported through the system.
  • Tighter, more efficient budgets — flying hour execution against real numbers instead of estimates.
  • Better-informed soldiers and commanders. Decisions happen against shared data in seconds, not walked over to ops.
06 What eight years there taught me

You can't design for a user you've never sat next to. Every meaningful decision in ASD traces back to something I watched at a unit — a workaround on a form, the way a whiteboard was oriented, the order someone read information off a notecard at shift change. None of it shows up in a requirements document.

You also can't design once and walk away. Eight years was the right amount of time to ship, watch it fail in unexpected ways, sit back down with the units, and rebuild. The work I'm proudest of isn't the launch version — it's the second and third pass, after a few thousand soldiers told us what we got wrong.
